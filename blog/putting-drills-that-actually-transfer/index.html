Putting drills that actually transfer to the course

January 13, 2026 · about 6 min read

Transfer comes from three trains: start line at short range, speed at long range, and a repeatable routine you use in drills and on the course. Here is how to stack them without turning putting into busywork.

Putting practice fails for boring reasons. People hit a lot of six footers with no gate, ignore lag putting, and never practice the routine they claim they use on the course. Then they miss a four footer on 18 and blame “hands.”

If you want a tight indoor option, pair this article with indoor putting mat routine: 20 minutes. If you want pressure habits that carry, read pressure practice: simulate the back nine after you can start the ball on line most of the time.

The three trains

Train 1: start line

If the ball does not start on your intended line, alignment tricks will not save you. Short range gates teach face and path without needing a coach’s eyes.

Train 2: speed

Three putts are usually speed mistakes that look like line mistakes. Long lag work trains length control and tempo.

Train 3: routine

A routine is not superstition. It is a sequence that narrows your attention before a stressful stroke.

Gate drill that does not lie

Set two tees or objects slightly wider than a ball, about 6 to 12 inches in front of the ball on your start line. Hit putts through the gate from 3 to 6 feet.

Rules:

  • If you hit a tee, it counts as a miss, even if the ball goes in
  • Do ten in a row from 3 feet before you move back
  • If you miss, reset the count

This is dull. It works.

The ladder for speed

Pick three distances on a real green or a long mat: for example 20, 35, and 50 feet if space allows. At each distance, hit five balls.

Scoring options:

  • Circle: 3 points inside a 3 foot circle, 1 point inside 6 feet, 0 outside 6 feet
  • Two putt rule: you “win” if you two putt or better, track wins out of five

Move distances each session so you do not memorize feel for one length only.

One routine, every rep

Pick a simple sequence, for example:

  1. Stand behind the ball, see the line, take a practice stroke in the air
  2. Set up, one look at the hole, one look at the ball
  3. Stroke

Use that sequence on every scored putt in practice, not only the “important” ones. If your practice strokes are lazy, your on course strokes will be lazy.

For a broader mental framework, mental routine before each shot on the range uses the same idea outside putting, but the putting routine should be shorter and repeatable.

Green reading without guessing

On a practice green, walk a putt before you hit it:

  • Stand behind the ball and read
  • Walk to the low side halfway and read again
  • Pick a start line and a speed

Even if you are wrong, you trained a process. Over time, compare your read to what the ball does and adjust your map.

The 10 foot test

Ten feet is a nasty distance because it feels makeable but is not automatic. Once a week, hit 20 putts from 10 feet with full routine. Track makes. Expect pros to be great here and amateurs to be streaky. What you want is trend, not ego.

Avoid these traps

Only practicing straight putts. Find breaking putts on purpose.

Rapid fire. One look, one stroke, next ball. That is not golf. It is a video game.

Changing putters every month. If you change equipment, rerun the gate drill so you know what the new face does.

Putting and journaling

If you keep a simple log after rounds, track:

  • Three putt holes
  • Missed short putts inside 6 feet
  • Long putts you left in the 4 to 8 foot “annoying zone”

Patterns show up fast. Journaling after rounds and range sessions gives a light template so you do not turn notes into a novel.

Sample week

Monday (20 minutes): gate drill + 10 foot test

Wednesday (20 minutes): speed ladder

Friday (20 minutes): gate at 6 feet + breaking putts only

Sunday pre round (10 minutes): gate + three lag putts with routine only

When you are lost

If everything feels shaky, shrink to gate only for a week. Rebuild start line. Then add speed. Golfers love to fix lag when the face is open at impact. Fix the first problem first.

Connection to short game days

Putting is still part of short game. If you have a 60 minute short game block, you can end with 15 minutes of putting after chips. The plan in short game practice plan for a focused 60 minutes pairs well with a dedicated putting micro session on another day.

The honesty rule

If you will not count makes, you are rehearsing motion, not skill. Motion matters, but putting is scored in strokes. Build drills with a number at the end, even a small one.

Green reading days

Once a month, spend half a putting session only walking lines, no stroke changes. Read, then hit one putt, compare prediction to outcome.

Breakers inside six feet

Practice slight breakers, not only straight putts. Straight putts lie to you about face control at the hole.

Eyes quiet

Some players peek early. Video can show it. Fix with a gate and a rule: listen for ball contact before looking up.

Link to monthly calendar

Putting micro sessions belong on the calendar like anything else. See building a monthly practice calendar.

Tournament week

Bias speed control and short start line, not mechanical overhaul.

Left hand low trials

If you experiment with grips, isolate changes to putting only and track makes for three weeks before judging.

Grain and dew

Morning dew changes speed. Afternoon firmness changes speed. Note time of day when you practice.

Aim tool usage

Mirrors and laser aim tools can help setup. Do not become dependent on tools you cannot bring on course unless you also train without them.

Fun finish

End with one long breaking putt for fun. Joy is allowed after scored work.

Recap

Start line work with a gate teaches face and path truth at short range. Speed work with a ladder teaches length control that stops three putt drama. A repeatable routine ties both together so the first tee and the eighteenth green use the same brain pathway. Indoor mats can support two of those three if you respect speed differences when you move outside. Pressure rules make the stroke honest. Journaling connects patterns across rounds so you stop fixing the wrong problem. Short game days should still include puttout when possible because a chip is only half the hole. If you feel lost, shrink to gate only for a week, then add speed again. If you feel great, keep scoring anyway so great days become repeatable instead of mysterious.

Transfer is not magic. It is start line plus speed plus a routine you actually use. Stack those three trains every week, keep the scores, and let the trend tell you what to work on next.

FAQ

How long should putting practice be?

Twenty focused minutes three times a week beats one long random hour. Consistency matters more than volume.

Should I use a line on the ball?

If it helps you see start line at short range, use it in practice. On course, use what you trust. The goal is a consistent start line, not a brand of marker.

What if I do not have a practice green?

A mat with a gate drill and a cup or coin for speed games still trains stroke and impact. You will add green reading on course days.
