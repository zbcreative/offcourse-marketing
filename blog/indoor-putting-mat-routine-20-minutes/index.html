Indoor putting mat routine (20 minutes)

February 12, 2026 · about 6 min read

Twenty minutes at home can save strokes if you repeat the same structure: gate reps, a mid range speed ladder, and a tiny pressure rule at the end.

Home putting is easy to half do while watching TV. This routine is for the other mode: training.

Pair it with putting drills that actually transfer for the full picture. If you want nerves, end with a rule from pressure practice: simulate the back nine.

Minute map

0 to 6: gate at short range

Two objects as a gate 6 to 12 inches ahead on your line. Roll 20 putts from 3 to 4 feet. Count how many pass clean.

6 to 12: start line with alignment check

Use one ball line or a chalk line if you have it. Ten putts from 5 to 6 feet with the same full routine you use on course. Focus on start direction, not makes only.

12 to 18: speed ladder

At three distances your mat allows, five balls each. Score:

  • 3 points inside a small target (cup, coin, tape box)
  • 1 point close
  • 0 points bad lag

18 to 20: pressure rule

Pick one:

  • Five in a row through the gate from 3 feet
  • Or two putts in a row stopping inside a 2 foot circle from your longest mat distance

If you miss, restart. Short timer, high focus.

Setup details

Ball position: consistent

Posture: slight forward tilt, eyes over or slightly inside ball depending on your fit

Grip pressure: light enough that the face can release

Sound and roll

On mats, listen for skid versus roll. Mats lie sometimes, but you can still learn a quiet roll pattern.

When the mat is slow or fast

Name your home speeds honestly. “Mat fast” means you respect speed on real greens later. Write a one line note after sessions in journaling after rounds and range sessions.

Weekly connection

This routine fits the micro sessions in a weekly golf practice routine that fits a busy schedule.

Avoid

Thirty different drills in twenty minutes.

Only short putts. Speed matters.

Rushing because it is home. Use the same routine cadence you want on course.

Travel version

A portable mat and a few balls in a hotel can save a trip. Keep the gate idea with anything that stands upright safely.

Link to short game days

Putting supports chipping when you actually putt out in practice. Blend indoor putting with short game practice plan for a focused 60 minutes on course days.

Metric to track weekly

Gate pass rate out of 20, and ladder points out of 15. Two numbers. Three weeks. You will see a trend.

If your mat breaks left or right

Mats can have subtle tilt. Learn your mat bias and do not confuse it with stroke bias. When you get to real grass, trust your routine first, then adjust read.

Stance and ball position checklist

Heels shoulder width for many players, ball slightly forward of center for most straight putts, eyes consistent. Small setup changes move start line more than people admit.

Eyes closed test

For five putts mid session, close eyes at address, stroke, then open to see result. It trains face and path feel without visual chasing. Use this sparingly if it makes you dizzy or unsafe.

Parent and kid note

If you share space with family, pick a time box and a noise rule. Twenty quiet minutes beats sixty interrupted minutes.

Link to full putting plan

Rotate this indoor block with putting drills that actually transfer so you keep lag and green reading in the mix when you have course access.

When you travel

Pack a small mat if you can. Hotel carpet plus a coin gate still trains start line. Something is better than full days with zero stroke reps.

Seasonal winter plan

Winter indoor months are a good time to raise your gate standard. If you pass 18 of 20 through the gate for two weeks straight, tighten the gate slightly or move back one foot.

Avoid the scroll

Put the phone face down. The point is twenty minutes of attention. Half attention trains half habits.

Tie to weekly routine

This fits the micro session idea in a weekly golf practice routine that fits a busy schedule. It is how Tuesday stays useful when you cannot touch grass.

Lighting

Dim light changes depth perception a little. If you practice at night, keep a consistent lamp position so your eyes learn one environment.

Carpet grain

Some carpets have direction. Learn if your putts break toward the couch every time. That is mat training bias, not stroke truth.

Pets and kids

Keep balls away from toddlers and dogs. Practice stops fast if someone chokes.

Alignment mirror optional

A small mirror can check shoulder aim. Do not get stuck staring. One look, then trust.

Link to pressure

Once a week, add a silly consequence: “If I miss three in a row, I do ten pushups.” Light stakes train focus. Bigger versions live in pressure practice: simulate the back nine.

Winter to spring transition

When you move back outside, expect speed differences. Re calibrate lag feel over two sessions.

Walkthrough: lunch break at home

Set a timer for twenty minutes. Six minutes gate, six minutes speed ladder at two distances only, six minutes pressure streak, two minutes easy rolls. If the timer ends mid streak, note where you stopped and restart there tomorrow. Consistency beats heroic random length sessions.

Neighbors and noise

If you live above someone, use a putting disc or softer ball if available. Good relationships beat perfect reps.

Distance illusion

Indoor rooms can make six feet feel longer or shorter than outdoor greens. Expect a recalibration day when you return outside. That is normal, not failure.

Grip pressure checks

Mid session, shake hands out, reset grip soft. Death grip steals face feel.

Routine on every length

Do not use routine only on short putts. Lag putts need a shorter version of the same idea from mental routine before each shot on the range, adapted for putting.

Eyes and glasses

If you got new lenses, expect depth perception shifts. Give it two weeks before you panic about reads.

Carpet seams

Put parallel to seams one week, across seams another week if you want robustness. Not mandatory, just advanced.

Partner practice

Two mats side by side, same drill, compare gate scores. Keep it friendly.

Monthly sanity

If indoor numbers look amazing and course numbers do not, bias outdoor speed work next month. Indoor trained start line, outdoor still owns speed.

Simple reward

End a streak week with a silly reward. Small treats keep habits alive.

Recap

Twenty structured minutes beat sixty distracted minutes. Gate, speed ladder, short pressure, done. Respect mat bias and recalibrate when you move outdoors. Keep routine consistent across lengths. Protect neighbors and sanity with reasonable noise and balls. Log a number weekly. Pair with full putting drills when you have green access. Indoor work is real training, not a toy version of golf, as long as you keep score and repeat.

Indoor putting is not a toy version of golf. It is reps without weather excuses. Keep the structure tight, keep the time short, and the course putts get a little less scary.

FAQ

What mat length do I need?

Longer is better for speed, but you can train start line on short mats too. Use available space and adapt distances.

How often?

Three to four times a week beats one long session, if you can manage it.

Will indoor putting hurt my green reading?

It does not replace green reading. It trains stroke and speed. Keep reading practice on real greens when you can.
