Wedge gapping session at the range (that you can repeat)

January 15, 2026 · about 5 min read

A gapping session is not hitting pretty shots. It is a measured map of carry distances for your scoring clubs, including three quarter swings if you use them. Repeat the same test monthly so changes in speed or shafts show up in the data.

Wedge gapping is how you stop guessing between a “hard sand wedge” and a “soft gap wedge” when the pin is middle front and trouble is long. You are building a carry map, not chasing feel good swings.

This session fits inside our one-hour range session template if you treat gapping as the narrow block for the day. If your full swing patterns are messy, fix low point basics first with ideas from deliberate practice versus beating balls.

What you need

  • A range with visible targets or flags you can pace off if needed
  • A notebook or phone notes
  • Wedges you actually bag, usually pitching wedge through lob wedge
  • Optional: alignment stick or club for feet lines

Warm-up (10 to 12 minutes)

Easy swings with a short iron. Then hit 10 wedges to a safe target without scoring. Goal: contact and tempo, not distance hunting.

The test format

For each club, you want carry estimates, not total distance with rollout that changes with green firmness.

If you cannot see carry precisely, use a consistent rule:

  • Note the target you cleared or landed near
  • Use the same landing window each time
  • Call “short,” “good,” or “long” immediately after each shot

Hit 6 to 8 balls per club, delete the worst mishit if you want, but do not delete all bad ones. Mishits are information.

Write:

  • Club name
  • Average carry feel (a number if you know it, otherwise A B C spacing)
  • Miss pattern (left, right, heavy, thin)

Order that reduces fatigue bias

Hit in this order to avoid always being fresh on lob wedge and tired on pitching wedge:

  1. Full swings from longest wedge to shortest (for example 48 degree to 58 degree)
  2. Then partial swings on your two favorite scoring clubs (for example 56 full, 56 three quarter, 52 three quarter)

Partial swings matter because many players use them more on course than they practice.

Define partial swings honestly

Use positions you can repeat:

  • Full: normal stock
  • Three quarter: shorter swing with same tempo (not decel)
  • Half: feel shot for short yardages, still with committed speed

Name them what you want, but write what you actually do, not what you wish you did.

Turn numbers into decisions

On course, you pick a club and a swing based on carry first, then adjust for wind and slope. If your map says your gap wedge carries 105 on a stock swing today, you do not choose it for a 115 pin over water unless you have a different shot in the bag.

If driver dispersion is your bigger problem, balance your week with driver range practice without swinging blind.

Repeat monthly

Grooves wear, bodies change, and speed changes. A monthly repeat session keeps the map honest. You are looking for drift, not perfection.

Track trends in tracking progress: what to measure off the course so gapping is not a one time event.

Common mistakes

Chasing max distance. Gapping is about predictable carry, not one range rocket.

Mixing ball types between sessions. Same range, same ball bucket where possible.

No routine between balls. Use the same pre shot routine for each measured shot.

If you have a launch monitor

Great. Still keep the same structure. Export or write:

  • Club
  • Average carry
  • Std deviation if available (tight dispersion is the point)

Short game link

Gapping is full swing spacing. Around the green, landing matters more than carry labels. Pair this session with short game practice plan for a focused 60 minutes so your short pitches match your full wedge map.

When gapping goes wrong

If distances jump around wildly, you may be:

  • Changing ball position
  • Changing face aim without noticing
  • Swinging harder when you see a far flag

Shrink to one club until the pattern stabilizes, then expand.

Wedge gapping is adult homework. It is also free strokes for players who stop guessing in the scoring zone.

Wind, elevation, and real course notes

Your range map is a baseline, not a law. Into wind, expect more club. Downwind, expect less. Uphill approaches play longer in decision making even when the number says something friendly. Write a second column on your sheet called “course adjustment” once you learn your local tendencies.

If you play multiple courses with big elevation change, keep one sheet per home course you play most. Trying to remember every adjustment in your head is how people default back to guessing.

When to re-gap during the season

Re-check wedges when you change balls, change shafts, feel a swing change stick, or notice your iron distances drifting. You do not need a full session weekly. You need a signal that spacing changed.

Pairing with on-course tests

Once a month, on a quiet hole or in a practice round, pick three scoring yardages you face often. Hit the shot you think is correct based on your map. Mark where it finishes relative to the pin. Adjust notes if the pattern repeats.

Sharing data with a coach

If you work with a coach, send a photo of your sheet plus your miss bias notes. It speeds lessons because you arrive with facts, not vibes.

If you only have partial swings

Some players live in three quarter wedges. Gap those swings on purpose, not only full swings. Write “56 full” and “56 3/4” as separate lines. On course, you want a default for each, not a debate at the top of your backswing.

Quick sanity check

If two adjacent clubs fly nearly the same distance, you have a gapping problem or a swing inconsistency problem. A coach can help you tell which. Your job in practice is to notice the cluster with honest samples, not one lucky pair on a downwind day.

Closing thought

Players who gap wedges stop asking the caddie in their head impossible questions. They pick a club, accept variance, and get back to committing. That is the real win, even more than the pretty chart.

Recap

Gapping maps carry distances for scoring clubs using repeated samples and honest mishit rules. Repeat monthly or when equipment or speed changes. Note range ball context so on course decisions stay humble. Pair gapping with short game landing work because carry labels do not replace green reading. Keep driver and wedge weeks balanced across a month. Write a simple chart, pencil first. Share with a coach if you have one. The goal is fewer guesses from 40 to 90 yards, not perfect ego numbers.

FAQ

Do I need a launch monitor?

No. You need a consistent range ball flight, a target you trust, and honest pacing. A monitor helps, but you can gap wedges well without one if you repeat the same process.

How many balls is enough?

Plan 60 to 90 balls. You want multiple samples per club, not one lucky shot per wedge.

What if range balls fly shorter than course balls?

Write “range numbers” on your sheet and use them for spacing decisions at that range. On course, confirm key yardages when you can with a real ball.
