A weekly golf practice routine that fits a busy schedule

January 7, 2026 · about 6 min read

You do not need six range days to improve. You need a weekly skeleton that repeats, even if each block is short. Here is how to split work, protect one anchor session, and stop reinventing the plan every Sunday night.

Most golfers do not fail because they lack motivation. They fail because the plan is too fragile. One late meeting, one sick kid, one bad weather day, and the whole week feels like a zero. A good weekly routine is boring on purpose. It survives real life.

This article is about structure, not heroics. If you want a full range template for a single visit, read our one-hour range session template. If you want the bigger picture across a month, see building a monthly practice calendar.

Start with non negotiables

Pick one anchor you will protect most weeks. For many players, that is a 60 to 90 minute range or short game block on the weekend. Name it, put it on the calendar like a meeting, and treat a reschedule as a reschedule, not a cancellation.

Then add two micro sessions in the 20 to 35 minute range. These are not “full practice.” They are maintenance. Examples:

  • Putting on a mat at home with one gate or one distance ladder
  • Chipping into a bucket in the yard with one club
  • Wedges at the range with a tight target and a ball count cap

If you only have two days total, merge ideas: 10 minutes of mobility, 40 minutes of focused work, 10 minutes of cooldown, done.

Split the week by job

Think in jobs, not clubs. Each job answers a different question.

Start lines and face control show up when you need to start the ball on a line you chose. Low point control shows up when your contact is thin or fat. Speed shows up when you three putt or blow chips long. Creativity and feel show up when you are good on the range and lost on the course.

A simple 3 day skeleton:

  1. Full swing day (range or simulator): one main theme, limited ball count, measured reps
  2. Short game day: chips and pitches with one scoring game
  3. Putting day: start line work plus lag work, even if the “lag” space is smaller than you want

If you add a fourth day, duplicate the weakest area for that month.

The Tuesday Thursday weekend model

Here is a pattern that works for a lot of employed golfers:

Tuesday (25 to 35 minutes): putting or chipping at home or at the course. One drill, one score, same drill for two weeks.

Thursday (25 to 35 minutes): wedges or partial swings if you have space, otherwise putting volume with a pressure rule (for example, you cannot quit until you hit ten two putts in a row from 25 feet on a mat).

Saturday or Sunday (60 to 90 minutes): range session with a written plan. If you tend to wear yourself out early, split 45 minutes range and 30 minutes short game.

Swap days as needed. Do not throw away the shape of the week.

Write the plan like a recipe

Before the week starts, five lines on paper is enough:

  • Theme for the long session (example: “driver start line”)
  • Ball budget (example: “60 balls max”)
  • Success metric (example: “7 of 10 start inside a 15 yard wide window”)
  • Short session drill name
  • One “if I only have 15 minutes” fallback (putting gate, 20 chips to a towel)

That last line matters more than people admit. It keeps you from doing nothing on the hard weeks.

Volume without stupidity

Busy golfers often swing hard once a week and wonder why their back disagrees. If your anchor is long, keep Monday or Wednesday as a real off day or walking only day. If you add speed work, treat it like lifting: hard day, easy day, not hard every day.

Rotate drills, not structure

Keep the weekly shape for at least three weeks. Inside each block, rotate the drill every two to three weeks, or when you clear a goal. If you change everything every session, you never learn what actually moved the needle.

What progress looks like on a calendar

You are not chasing a feeling every week. You are chasing evidence:

  • Did the anchor session happen?
  • Did you log a number, even a simple one?
  • Did you avoid the classic trap of “I hit a large bucket with no target”?

Tracking can be a notebook, a notes app, or a tool like Offcourse. If you want metrics that matter off the course, tracking progress: what to measure gives you a short list that does not turn into spreadsheet hell.

Common mistakes

Mystery Monday planning. You roll up to the range, pay, then think about what to do. That is how you default to driver only.

All or nothing language. If you miss Tuesday, you do not “ruin the week.” You do Thursday as written.

No short game for months. Full swing is fun. Short game is bankable. If you are time poor, you can argue short game should be more than half of your micro sessions.

Ignoring pressure. If every rep is casual, the first tee still feels new. Once a week, add one rule that makes you care about the score. Pressure practice: simulate the back nine is a good add when you have a longer block.

A sample month of themes

You can keep the same weekly skeleton and shift themes:

  • Weeks 1 to 2: wedge start lines from 50 to 80 yards
  • Weeks 3 to 4: driver face strike and start line (same range template, different intent)
  • Ongoing: putting start line at 6 feet, four days a week if possible, even if two days are only 10 minutes

Close the loop

Once a month, ask three questions:

  1. What shot cost me the most strokes recently?
  2. Did my practice blocks address that job, or did I avoid it because it is annoying?
  3. What is the smallest change to next month’s skeleton that makes the honest answer to question 2 “yes”?

You are allowed to enjoy practice. You are also allowed to make it predictable. Busy golfers improve when the plan is small enough to survive a bad week and honest enough to face their real weaknesses.

If you want fresh ideas for the range day itself, bookmark range games for boring buckets for the weeks when motivation dips.

Recap

Busy golfers need a weekly skeleton that repeats: anchors, micro sessions, and clear jobs. Protect one longer block, keep two short touchpoints, rotate drills inside stable structure. Track something small monthly so you know if the skeleton is working. Short game deserves real minutes, not leftovers. Pressure belongs sometimes, not always. Deloads belong seasonally. The win is consistency across months, not one perfect week that falls apart when life gets loud.

FAQ

How many days per week should I practice if I work full time?

Three honest touchpoints beat one marathon. Aim for two short midweek blocks plus one longer weekend block when you can. If you only have two days, make one full swing and one short game or putting.

What if I miss a whole week?

Pick up the same template next week. Progress is about months of repeats, not one perfect week. Log what you did and move on.

Should I practice the same thing every week?

Keep the structure stable (for example, Tuesday wedges, Thursday putting, Saturday range). Rotate the specific drill inside each block every two to three weeks.
