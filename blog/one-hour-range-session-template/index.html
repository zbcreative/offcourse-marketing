One-hour range session template that does not waste balls

January 9, 2026 · about 6 min read

A good hour has four parts: prep, a narrow technical block, a test that looks like golf, and a short cooldown. Here is a template you can repeat, with ball counts that stop you from grinding yourself dull.

An hour at the range is easy to waste. You hit a few wedges to “loosen up,” then drift into driver because it feels good, then realize you have ten minutes left and no plan. This template fixes the order, caps the ball count, and forces one honest test before you leave.

If you are building a full week around a busy job, pair this with a weekly golf practice routine that fits a busy schedule. If you need wedge specifics, jump to wedge gapping session at the range.

The four parts

0 to 12 minutes: prep and pattern

Goal: tissue warm, not swing heroics. Walk, easy rotations, a few step swings without a ball if you have space. Then small swings with a short iron or wedge with a low stake target. This is not scoring yet. It is contact and tempo.

12 to 35 minutes: one narrow block

Pick one theme only. Examples:

  • Start line with a mid iron using an intermediate target two feet in front of the ball
  • Low point control with a line of chalk or a tee on the mat side of the ball
  • Driver face strike with tee height held constant and a goal of centered feel

Use 30 to 45 balls in this block. Work in sets of 5 with a walk, a breath, and a rehearsal between sets. If you want a deeper take on why this beats random full swings, read deliberate practice versus beating balls.

35 to 50 minutes: test

Make it look like golf, not like range hero ball. Examples:

  • 9 balls: 3 different “fairways” you draw in your mind, 3 different distances you pretend, 3 different shot shapes if you can do it safely
  • 10 drivers: each one has a “first tee” routine, and you score fairway width you defined before you start
  • 8 wedges: alternate distances, no re dos, write the result after each

50 to 60 minutes: cooldown

10 to 12 easy swings with a wedge, or slow motion rehearsals, or putting strokes without a ball if you are indoors. The goal is to leave without a rushed last swing.

Ball math that works

A practical split for 70 balls total:

  • Prep: 10 to 15 balls with short clubs
  • Block: 40 balls on one theme
  • Test: 15 to 20 balls
  • Cooldown: optional last few with a wedge

If you only buy a small bucket, cut the block to 25 balls and keep the test.

Picking the theme for the day

Use last round or last range day. Ask: what cost strokes?

  • Two way miss off the tee: bias driver start line and face in the block
  • Good strikes, bad direction: aim process and start line work
  • Fat irons: low point and weight shift patterns with shorter swings first
  • Wedges short and long: gapping and distance control, not more full swings

For tee shots specifically, driver range practice without swinging blind adds structure when you tend to default to “max only.”

Targets that actually teach

You need two layers of target:

  1. An intermediate target a foot or two in front of the ball for start line
  2. A distant target for curvature and overall intent

If you only pick the far target, you will lie to yourself about where the ball started.

The scorecard rule

Before the test block, write one number you will compute at the end. Examples:

  • Fairways hit out of 10 drivers using a 30 yard wide “fairway”
  • Greens or “acceptable misses” out of 8 irons using a range flag and a circle you define
  • Proximity score for wedges using a point system (3 points inside a towel, 1 point on the towel, 0 points long or short)

No score, no truth. If you want playful structure when you are bored, add ideas from range games for boring buckets.

What to avoid in a one hour window

Speeding up when you get tired. Fatigue is when patterns get sloppy. When the block ends, it ends.

Fixing everything. One theme. If your brain says “also my grip,” write it on a list for another day.

No walk between sets. Standing still and rapid firing is how you practice the wrong thing with confidence.

Indoor and simulator notes

If you are on a simulator, treat the block as metric based (face, path, low point if available) but still end with a test that uses on course visuals. Simulators teach numbers. Golf asks you to see a shot. Blend both.

After the session: 60 seconds of logging

Write:

  • Theme
  • Ball count
  • Test score
  • One sentence on what you will repeat next time
  • One sentence on what you will change

That log turns random range days into a system. For more ways to measure without drowning in data, see tracking progress: what to measure off the course.

Sample week rotation

  • Week 1 hour 1: mid iron start line block, 8 iron test
  • Week 1 hour 2 (if you have two hours this week): wedges, gapping test
  • Week 2 hour 1: driver block, fairway test
  • Week 2 hour 2: short game at a practice green if you can, not more full swing

You do not need perfect weeks. You need repeatable hours that do not dissolve into driver only entertainment.

FAQ tie in

If you are unsure how often to repeat the same theme, a simple rule is three sessions on one theme before you judge it. Bodies learn slower than egos want.

Use this template as a default. When life gives you 30 minutes, cut the test block in half and keep the narrow block. Something measured always beats something heroic.

When the range is cold, wet, or crowded

Bad conditions are not an excuse to erase structure. They are a reason to shrink the test and protect the block.

If mats are slick, bias the block toward shorter swings and low point work you can feel even when spin is weird. If you cannot see ball flight well, lean on start line at short distance and sound or feel at impact. If bays are full and you feel rushed, pick one club for the whole narrow block so you are not adjusting the bag every five balls.

Crowding also kills rest between swings. Step back, count to five, and pretend you are waiting on a par 3 in a tournament. Rest is part of the skill.

Pairing the hour with a mental habit

You can tack a mental layer onto the test block without turning the day into therapy. Before each shot in the test, pick a single cue (for example, “smooth transition” or “finish tall”). After the shot, grade only whether you committed to the cue, not whether you loved the result. That trains pre shot focus in a low drama way. For a deeper version of this idea, read mental routine before each shot on the range.

After a bad round

If yesterday was rough, resist the urge to “fix” five things in one hour. Pick the one miss that showed up under pressure, build the narrow block around that job, and keep the test simple. Progress after a bad round is often emotional steadiness, not mechanical overhaul.

When you finish early

If you run through the plan with time left, do not invent a new theme. Repeat the test with a stricter rule, or add a second score on the same test. Depth beats novelty in a 60 minute window.

FAQ

Is 60 balls enough for a real session?

Yes, if each ball has a job. Most amateurs improve faster with 60 mindful balls than 120 casual ones.

Should I hit driver in every session?

Not every session. If your miss is costing strokes off the tee, bias the plan toward driver for two to three weeks, then rebalance.

What if the range is packed and I cannot use my usual stations?

Shrink the test block, keep the prep and the narrow block, and pick one measurable target you can see from any mat.
