Driver is the loudest club in the bag. It makes noise, it flies far, and it tempts you to ignore the scorecard story that says you lost three balls right. This is a practice frame for **fairways**, not Instagram.

Use [our one-hour range session template](/blog/one-hour-range-session-template/) and make driver the narrow block, or split a long session between driver and [wedge gapping session at the range](/blog/wedge-gapping-session-at-the-range/).

## Start with a window, not a range flag

Pick a **fairway width** before you hit. Example: 30 yards between two posts or two trees you identify. If you cannot see width well, use a small mat alignment and a start line gate for the first third of the session.

Write the window down. “Fairway” is not a vibe. It is a width you can see.

## Block A: start line (8 to 12 balls)

Tee height consistent. Aim process consistent. Goal: start the ball through a gate of tees 12 to 18 inches ahead. Ignore distance. Ignore shape unless shape is your theme for the month.

If you miss the gate, fix setup first:

- Ball position too far forward can add slice spin for some players
- Aim can be “correct” to the flag and still wrong for your pattern

Start line work is boring. It is also the fastest way to stop lying to yourself about “I aimed wrong” when the face was open.

## Block B: centered strike (10 to 15 balls)

If you have foot spray or face tape, use it. If not, listen and feel for high face, low face, heel, toe. Hit with **80 to 90 percent** speed. Center first, speed later.

Heel strikes often curve more one way. Toe strikes often curve the other. Your job is not to moralize. Your job is to see a pattern.

If you want athletic prep before speed work, read [speed training safely for more distance](/blog/speed-training-safely-for-more-distance/) and keep it off the same day as heavy driver volume at first.

## Block C: fairway test (10 to 12 balls)

Each shot:

- Stand behind, pick line, one rehearsal
- Commit
- Call result in the air: fairway or miss, left or right

Score fairways out of 10 or 12. Write it down.

If you want variety, alternate tee heights every two balls while keeping the same window. The point is to practice **decisions**, not one perfect setup only.

## The one mistake rule

If you hit two big misses in a row, drop to **half swings** for three balls, then build back up. You are training panic control, not stubborn repetition.

## Shape practice days

If you need a cut or draw for your home course, dedicate one session to **one** shape only. Do not blend draw fixes and fade fixes in the same hour unless a coach asked you to.

Shape days still use a window. A draw that starts straight and ends in the left rough is not a draw you own.

## Mental piece

Driver mistakes grow when you rush. A simple pre shot routine belongs here just like putting. Borrow structure from [mental routine before each shot on the range](/blog/mental-routine-before-each-shot-on-range/).

## Pressure

Once a month, run a driver test with a consequence story: “This is 18, tied match, tight fairway.” You do not need real money. You need a brain state. [Pressure practice: simulate the back nine](/blog/pressure-practice-simulate-the-back-nine/) helps you build that state on purpose.

## Common range lies

**Wind at your back every time.** Note wind. Practice into wind sometimes even if it is humbling.

**Always elevated mats.** If mats make you flatter or steeper, note it. Compare to grass when you can.

**Chasing carry on the screen.** If your range has metrics, use them as feedback, not as a personality test.

## Logging

After the session:

- Fairway test score
- Strike pattern in one sentence
- One swing thought that helped, one that did not

Patterns over four sessions beat feelings after one bucket.

## Weekly placement

If you play once a week, **one** driver block per week is enough for many mid handicaps, as long as short game and putting still get time. See [a weekly golf practice routine that fits a busy schedule](/blog/weekly-golf-practice-routine-busy-schedule/).

## When distance is the goal

Some weeks you should practice **max**. Do it as its own block with a different success metric (carry, spin, landing area). Fairway practice and max practice can live in the same month, not always in the same ten minutes.

## Tie it to on course stats

If you journal, track penalties and recovery shots off the tee. [Journaling after rounds and range sessions](/blog/journaling-after-rounds-and-range-sessions/) helps you aim driver practice at real leaks, not imagined ones.

## Tee height experiments

Once a month, test one click of tee height change while keeping the same window. Sometimes strike improves with tiny setup tweaks. Log results.

## Alignment stick on the ground

A stick parallel to target line for feet and ball position can train consistency. Remove it for the test block if you want pure on course visuals.

## Wind days

Into wind, smaller window practice helps. Downwind, control spin and flight height with a coach’s guidance if you fight balloons.

## Pair with wedge days

Great drives mean nothing if wedges are random. Keep [wedge gapping session at the range](/blog/wedge-gapping-session-at-the-range/) in the monthly mix.

## Fatigue management

If you cannot balance after ten drivers, you are done for the day on speed drivers. Finish with smooth wedges.

## Group practice etiquette

Do not hog the end mat because you like the view. Share space. Good karma improves focus, probably.

## When to get help

If dispersion is wild with centered feel, a lesson or club check can save months of guessing.

## Routine on the first tee

Use the same pre shot routine here as on course. [Mental routine before each shot on the range](/blog/mental-routine-before-each-shot-on-range/) applies to tee shots too.

## Honest scoring

If you count a ball as fairway when it is in the first cut, you are only cheating your map. Pick a definition and hold it.

## Walkthrough: thirty minute driver only

Warm up without driver first. Then twelve balls start line gate, twelve balls centered strike at eighty percent, ten ball fairway test with full routine. Write fairway score. Next session, same test. If the score jumps around with no pattern, check aim and ball position before you change your swing.

## Tee box clutter

Remove extra clubs from your station if they make you fidget. Fewer objects, cleaner decisions.

## Recap

Define a fairway window, train start line and strike, then test with a score. Stop when quality drops. Pair driver work with wedge planning so distance does not outrun scoring skills. Use a routine. Add pressure sometimes. Log fairway rate trends. Respect wind and mats as context, not excuses. Seek help if dispersion and feel disagree for weeks. Driver practice is adult, boring, and scoreable. That combo is how tee boxes get quieter.

Driver practice is not “hit until you feel good.” It is **define fairway, hit with intent, score, adjust**. Do that and the tee box gets quieter.
