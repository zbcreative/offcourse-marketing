Short game practice is where golfers tell the prettiest stories. “I chipped for an hour” often means twenty minutes of random flops and forty minutes of socializing. A real hour has segments, a score, and a stop time.

This plan assumes you have a chipping green or a safe area where you can fly the ball a short distance and let it roll. If you are space limited, shrink distances but keep the **structure**.

Pair this with [chipping ladder drill variations](/blog/chipping-ladder-drill-variations/) when you want more games inside the landing segment. If sand is your stress point, add [bunker practice when the course is closed](/blog/bunker-practice-when-the-course-is-closed/) on another day rather than cramming everything into one hour.

## Minute map

**0 to 8: warm-up and feel**

A few quiet swings without a ball, then 10 to 12 small chips that are not scored. Goal: brush the turf or mat, predictable low flight if that is your stock shot.

**8 to 22: landing zone block**

Pick one club you actually use on the course (often a gap wedge, pitching wedge, or 9 iron for bump runs). Pick **one landing spot** (a coin, a leaf, a tee in the ground). Hit 20 balls trying to land on or within one club length of that spot. Count how many land in the zone. Write the number.

**22 to 38: distance control ladder**

Use two or three distances you face often, for example 10, 20, and 30 yards carry if space allows. Five balls each distance, no mulligans. Score proximity using a simple rule: 3 points inside a towel, 1 point touching the towel, 0 points if you are short sided long or short.

**38 to 52: pressure game**

Pick one:

- **Up and down:** chip from six different lies if you can find them, putt everything out if a cup exists. Score how many saves out of six.
- **Worst ball:** two balls from the same spot, take the worse result, repeat five stations.
- **Consecutive makes:** from one tough distance, chip until you get three inside a defined circle in a row.

**52 to 60: cooldown**

Easy chips with your safest swing, or practice the motion you want to feel on course tomorrow. No new technique.

## Choose the club on purpose

If you always practice the 60 degree wedge, you will always practice the 60 degree wedge on the course even when a 8 iron bump is smarter. For at least half your sessions, practice the **boring** club until boring becomes automatic.

## Landing zone details that matter

Most amateurs think about the hole first. Better players think **landing first**, then let roll do the job.

Pick a landing spot that matches the shot:

- Runner: land short, roll a lot
- Standard chip: land on the front third of the window you want
- Higher shot: land closer to you with more carry

If you do not know your carry distances with a wedge around the green, this hour is also a simple test. After the session, write one sentence: “My PW lands here when I make this swing.” That feeds directly into [wedge gapping session at the range](/blog/wedge-gapping-session-at-the-range/) when you move to full swings.

## Distance control without a laser

You do not need perfect yardages to practice control. You need **repeatable swings** at repeatable lengths.

A simple approach:

- **Short:** hands low, narrow stance, shorter backswing
- **Medium:** stock motion
- **Long:** wider stance, longer backswing, same tempo

Keep tempo similar. Change length. Many chunked chips come from speeding up a short swing.

## Pressure block: make it ugly on purpose

If you only practice flat lies, the course feels unfair. Find sidehill lies, tight lies, and fluffy lies if your facility allows. If not, change stance: ball back, ball forward, open face for one high shot, closed for one low shot. The point is **variety under rules**, not perfect lies only.

For nerves, borrow ideas from [pressure practice: simulate the back nine](/blog/pressure-practice-simulate-the-back-nine/). Even five minutes of “I must get up and down to save the round” acting changes how you rehearse.

## Common mistakes in a short game hour

**Too many swing thoughts.** One mechanical cue for the block, not six.

**No puttout.** If you chip without finishing, you practice half the shot.

**Chasing video moves** you saw online without a baseline. Film yourself if you want, but keep the scoring system stable for three weeks so you know if anything moved.

## Indoor and backyard versions

**Mat and bucket:** use a bucket at three distances. Land on the mat edge or a spot on the mat, roll toward the bucket. Score hits.

**Yard:** use a hula hoop or rope as a landing zone. If neighbors are close, use almost no hinge and tiny pitches.

## Log the session in three lines

1. Landing score out of 20
2. Ladder total points
3. Pressure game result

Next session, repeat the same test on week one. On week two, change one variable (different club, different lie mix) but keep the same test format. That is how you see real change.

## How this connects to full swing days

Short game hours do not replace range work. They complement it. A simple weekly split is range day plus short game day, which matches the skeleton in [a weekly golf practice routine that fits a busy schedule](/blog/weekly-golf-practice-routine-busy-schedule/).

If your putting is the leak, bias the end of the hour to puttout from longer distances, or add a dedicated putting day with [putting drills that actually transfer](/blog/putting-drills-that-actually-transfer/).

## When you only have 30 minutes

Do landing zone and pressure only. Cut the ladder. Still write a score. Half an hour with a number beats a wandering forty five minutes.

## Walkthrough: solo sixty on a quiet evening

Arrive, putt two balls to wake hands, then start the minute map as written. No phone. No swing tips videos mid session. If you finish early, repeat the pressure block with a harder rule, do not wander into full swings. Leave with three numbers written: landing, ladder, pressure. Next session, same structure until numbers move. If you need fresh games inside the ladder, borrow [chipping ladder drill variations](/blog/chipping-ladder-drill-variations/).

## Facility close time

If the course closes soon, do landing and pressure only. Still log scores.

## Recap

Sixty minutes needs segments: warm-up, landing, distance control, pressure, cooldown. Pick one main club for boring honesty. Land the ball on purpose before you worry about the hole. Score everything. Add pressure that fits your skill level. Puttout when you can. Log three numbers. Repeat tests weekly before you change drills. Blend with bunker and putting days across a month. Short game is where strokes hide and where practice pays quietly. If you only remember one idea, remember landing zones beat staring at the hole.

Short game rewards repetition and honesty. Keep the hour tight, keep the score, and let the ego take the hit when the number is bad. Bad numbers are good information. Random practice is no information at all.
