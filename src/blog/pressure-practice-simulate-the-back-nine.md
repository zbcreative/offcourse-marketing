Most golfers practice like the score does not matter, then wonder why the swing changes on 16 with a match on the line. Pressure practice makes the range feel a little more like the back nine on purpose.

Start with a stable routine from [mental routine before each shot on the range](/blog/mental-routine-before-each-shot-on-range/). Use [our one-hour range session template](/blog/one-hour-range-session-template/) and make the test block your pressure lab.

## Rule 1: define the scenario before you start

Examples:

- “Down one with three holes to play, need two pars and a birdie chance”
- “Must hit four fairways in a row to win”
- “Up and down or the round is over” on short game days

Write it down. Vague pressure is not pressure.

## Rule 2: streaks beat one off tries

Pick a task you can measure:

- 5 drivers in a defined fairway window in a row from [driver range practice without swinging blind](/blog/driver-range-practice-without-swinging-blind/)
- 10 consecutive two putts from 25 to 35 feet on a mat
- 6 chips inside a circle in a row

If you miss, reset to zero. This teaches emotional reset, not perfection.

## Rule 3: one mulligan per block, or none

Mulligans teach relief. Sometimes use **zero** mulligans so you live with outcomes. Sometimes allow **one** so you practice decision making about when to use it.

## Rule 4: time pressure

Set a timer for 20 minutes. Complete a full test inside it including routine. This fights the slow death of overthinking.

## Short game pressure

On a chipping green, play [short game practice plan for a focused 60 minutes](/blog/short-game-practice-plan-60-minutes/) but end with “must get up and down from three tough lies in a row.”

## Putting pressure

Use [putting drills that actually transfer](/blog/putting-drills-that-actually-transfer/) and add: “If I miss two inside six feet, I start the ladder again.”

## Social pressure without toxicity

Tell a friend your streak goal before you start. They do not need to watch the whole time. The act of saying it out loud changes attention.

## What not to do

**Shame spirals.** Pressure practice is training, not punishment. If you get angry, shorten the drill.

**Only pressure, never basics.** You still need quiet blocks for technique.

**Copying TV collapse drama.** You can care without theatrics.

## Tie to journaling

After a pressure session, one line: “What broke first, body or brain?” [Journaling after rounds and range sessions](/blog/journaling-after-rounds-and-range-sessions/) helps you see patterns across weeks.

## Monthly rhythm

Week 1: technique heavy

Week 2: technique plus light pressure

Week 3: pressure heavy

Week 4: deload or tournament week adjustments from [deload week when your body or scores stall](/blog/deload-week-when-your-body-or-scores-stall/)

## Simulator note

Simulators can add games. Keep one metric honest (start line, carry dispersion) so the game does not hide swing issues.

## Junior note

Keep stakes small and language positive. Pressure should feel like a game, not fear.

## Breathing under streak rules

When a streak breaks, exhale, walk in a small circle, restart. The skill you train is reset, not perfection. That maps to golf: bad holes happen, rounds continue.

## Voiceover trick

Quietly narrate your routine steps like you are teaching a junior. It slows the brain just enough to stop rushing.

## Scorekeeping with a partner

Agree on one metric only. Arguments about rules kill the session. Write rules on a phone note before you start.

## When pressure backfires

If you finish angry more than focused, reduce stakes for two weeks. Pressure is dosage. Too much is poison.

## Link to putting and wedges

Pressure is not only driver. Use the same streak idea on 6 footers or wedge ladders from [chipping ladder drill variations](/blog/chipping-ladder-drill-variations/).

## Course prep day

The day before an event, light pressure, mostly routine and feel. Hard pressure belongs mid cycle, not on the eve.

## Reflect after

One line in your journal: “What tightened, hands or breath?” See [journaling after rounds and range sessions](/blog/journaling-after-rounds-and-range-sessions/).

## Amateur mistake

Turning every range day into a crisis movie. You still need quiet technique blocks from [deliberate practice versus beating balls](/blog/deliberate-practice-vs-beating-balls/).

## Scenario library (steal these)

- “Must find fairway on 18 to win the league”
- “Buddy match, one down, three holes left”
- “Stripe show, you told everyone you fixed the slice”
- “First tee with strangers watching”

Pick one, read it aloud, then run your test. Silly works. Your brain responds to story.

## Heart rate note

If you wear a watch, notice HR during pressure games versus casual swings. The goal is not zero nerves. The goal is **usable** nerves.

## Recovery after pressure days

Pressure practice is still stress. Sleep and eat normally. If you stack pressure day with max lifting and a scramble event, expect fog.

## Link to indoor putting

Pressure translates indoors too. Use [indoor putting mat routine (20 minutes)](/blog/indoor-putting-mat-routine-20-minutes/) with a streak rule.

## When to teach juniors

Keep stakes tiny. Ice cream, not cash. Teach process praise: “You reset well after a miss.”

## Long term skill

Think of pressure practice like learning a language. Short frequent lessons beat one yearly panic cram.

## Walkthrough: twenty minute back nine sim

Pick three clubs you trust. Simulate three holes: tee shot, approach, wedge in. Each shot gets one routine, no mulligans. Write a score for each hole as if you were on course. Repeat weekly with the same clubs until the story feels boring, then change the story. Boring is when your heart rate stops spiking, which means you are learning.

## Hydration

Even pressure practice dehydrates you if you are amped. Drink water. Basic, real.

## Music off

Some players need silence to train pressure. Experiment.

## Scorecard language

Use golf language in your story: “must par in,” “bogey is okay here,” “cannot short side.” That maps to real decisions.

## Link to driver tests

Pressure pairs with fairway tests in [driver range practice without swinging blind](/blog/driver-range-practice-without-swinging-blind/).

## Link to putting

Two footers with a streak rule belong here too. Steal drills from [putting drills that actually transfer](/blog/putting-drills-that-actually-transfer/).

## Avoid humiliation culture

Pressure is training, not bullying. If a partner crosses a line, reset boundaries.

## Sleep before pressure day

Bad sleep plus high stakes practice can feel like panic. Choose your days.

## Reflection scale

After session, rate stress 1 to 10. Track if 6 to 7 becomes normal over months. That is growth.

## Seasonal timing

Spring jitters are real. Schedule pressure practice before first big outing.

## Recap

Pressure is trained with rules that make you care: streaks, scenarios, time limits, and honest scoring. Use it as part of a balanced month, not as a replacement for quiet technique work. Pair it with a stable routine so nerves have a path, not a blank. Keep stakes appropriate for your life and friendships. Hydrate, sleep, and treat pressure sessions like real output. Review trends over weeks, not one heroic night. Use driver tests, wedge games, and putting streaks so pressure is not only a long swing story. End sessions with a short cooldown so you do not carry tension into tomorrow. If it stops being useful, reduce dosage. The goal is familiarity, not constant adrenaline.

Pressure practice is not about being tough online. It is about **familiarity**. Make the range feel a little like the moments you care about, and the course feels a little more like the range.
