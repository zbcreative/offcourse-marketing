Bunkers punish golfers who only visit them on Saturday with a card in hand. A small amount of intentional practice makes sand boring, and boring is the goal.

Pair bunker work with [short game practice plan for a focused 60 minutes](/blog/short-game-practice-plan-60-minutes/) on days you can access sand early in the session, not when you are tired.

## Session length

15 to 25 minutes is enough for maintenance. 40 minutes if you are rebuilding confidence.

## The three checkpoints

**Checkpoint 1: entry point**

Pick a spot in the sand an inch or two behind the ball, depending on your conditions and coach guidance. Your job is to hit **sand first**, not ball first.

**Checkpoint 2: face open enough**

Many players aim square and wonder why the leading edge digs. Open stance and open face are common fixes, but follow what your coach teaches. Consistency beats copying a random video.

**Checkpoint 3: speed through**

Deceleration leaves the ball in the bunker. Practice swings should feel like the club finishes, not stabs.

## Progression

**Level 1:** no ball, sand splashes that feel consistent

**Level 2:** ball, short carry, big target

**Level 3:** ball, smaller target, varied lies if available

## Scoring

Count **outs** in a row: out of the bunker onto the green or fringe counts as success. Pick a defined circle if you want stricter scoring.

## Common faults

**Trying to pick it clean** because you are scared of sand contact. That is how skulls happen.

**Ball too far back** with a shut face. Sometimes it works once, then it fails loudly.

**Changing technique every swing.** Pick one theme for the day.

## When course is closed

Some facilities still allow practice bunkers. Ask. If not, travel once a month to a public range with sand. The trip is cheaper than a double bogey habit.

## Mental

Treat sand as a skill, not a character test. [Mental routine before each shot on the range](/blog/mental-routine-before-each-shot-on-range/) applies: one cue, commit, go.

## Link to chipping

Bunker shots are still short game. Alternating weeks between [chipping ladder drill variations](/blog/chipping-ladder-drill-variations/) and bunker outs keeps short game honest.

## Pressure

End with a streak rule: three outs in a row before you leave. Borrow intensity ideas from [pressure practice: simulate the back nine](/blog/pressure-practice-simulate-the-back-nine/).

## Notes after

Write one line:

- “Entry felt late” or “Face not open enough” or “Speed good”

That is enough for next time.

## Fitness tie in

Sand swings ask for rotation and wrist stability. If you feel beat up, check [golf mobility warm-up before you hit balls](/blog/golf-mobility-warm-up-before-you-hit-balls/) before practice and [deload week when your body or scores stall](/blog/deload-week-when-your-body-or-scores-stall/) if joints complain.

## Wet sand versus fluffy sand

If your course has both, practice both when you can. Entry and speed change. Write one note after each session about sand condition so you do not gaslight yourself later.

## The skull fear

Many players speed up because they fear the leading edge. Controlled speed through the sand is different from jerking the handle. If skulls happen, return to sand only swings with no ball until splashes look similar.

## Rake and etiquette

Replace rakes, smooth footprints, keep pace. Good etiquette means you get invited back to practice sand more often.

## Wedge bounce basics without jargon

Higher bounce can help in soft sand for some players. Lower bounce can help in firm sand for some players. If you do not know your wedges, ask a fitter or coach once, then stop shopping and start practicing.

## Video sparingly

Film one rep set monthly if you want. Do not film every swing unless you have a review habit. Video without review is just storage.

## Pair with journaling

Note sand saves in rounds. If you never face bunkers in play, practice still matters, but priority might be lower than short grass chipping. Be honest about your course.

## When the facility only allows short sand shots

That is fine. Short sand is the high frequency shot for many amateurs. Own it before you chase long explosion Hollywood shots.

## Weather windows

Some regions freeze practice bunkers. Plan a monthly sand trip when thaw hits, even if it is one session. Frequency beats intensity for fear reduction.

## Clubface awareness

If you tend to aim the face at the target like a putter, you may fight sand entry. This is coach territory, but journaling your aim habit helps lessons go faster. Tie to [journaling after rounds and range sessions](/blog/journaling-after-rounds-and-range-sessions/).

## Footwork

Stable feet help stable entry. If you slide, simplify stance width and slow tempo until splashes repeat.

## Practice with gloves on and off

Some people learn feel with glove off for a few reps, then return to glove for full sets. Hygiene and facility rules matter. Wash hands after.

## Pair with stats

If you track sand saves over time in [tracking progress: what to measure off the course](/blog/tracking-progress-what-to-measure-off-course/), you will see fear turn into numbers.

## When to choose chipping instead

If the lip is too high for your skill level, practicing the wrong bunker shot trains panic. Move to [chipping ladder drill variations](/blog/chipping-ladder-drill-variations/) that fit the course you play.

## Instructor drill of the week

If you work with a coach, film one minute of sand work monthly. Compare month to month for entry point consistency.

## Travel buckets

Some resorts have practice sand. Arrive ten minutes early on trip rounds. Cheap insurance.

## Walkthrough: fifteen outs

Goal: three consecutive outs from a basic lie. If you fail at two in a row, return to no ball splashes for five reps, then restart the streak count. Leave after fifteen good exits or after forty five minutes, whichever comes first. Write exit quality in one word: “heavy,” “thin,” “clean.” Next session, chase the word you did not like.

## Sunscreen and sand

Outdoor practice sand still burns skin. Hat and sunscreen matter for focus.

## Footprint discipline

Rake and smooth your area so your next visit starts fair. Sand practice includes citizenship.

## Club selection sanity

If you always practice with your highest loft, try one session with a lower loft sand wedge if your coach agrees. Variety reduces fear.

## Link to pressure

Streak outs belong in [pressure practice: simulate the back nine](/blog/pressure-practice-simulate-the-back-nine/) once basics feel stable.

## Quiet hands reminder

Many players over use hands when scared. Feel bigger body rotation with quiet wrists if that matches your instruction.

## Wet sand session

If available, one wet sand day changes speed needs. Note it.

## Posture

Slight knee flex stable, not squatting and standing mid swing.

## Eyes

Pick a spot in the sand you want to enter, not only the ball, if that matches your method.

## Celebrate outs

When you get three in a row, stop and smile. Brains learn from rewards.

## Recap

Short, frequent sand reps reduce panic more than rare marathon attempts. Train entry, face, and speed through the sand. Progress from no ball splashes to scored outs. Note sand conditions. Pair with chipping ladders over a month for balanced short game. Use pressure when basics feel stable. Respect etiquette and safety. If access is limited, travel occasionally for sand anyway. The goal is a checklist you trust when the lip looks tall.

Bunker practice is not glamorous. It is insurance. Short, frequent, scored reps turn panic into a checklist you can trust.
