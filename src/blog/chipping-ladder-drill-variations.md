Chipping ladders turn “I chipped for a while” into “I passed rung four.” They are simple, measurable, and easy to repeat.

Use them inside [short game practice plan for a focused 60 minutes](/blog/short-game-practice-plan-60-minutes/) as the landing or pressure segment. If sand is weak, still keep separate time for [bunker practice when the course is closed](/blog/bunker-practice-when-the-course-is-closed/).

## Basic ladder: distance rungs

Set three distances along the same line, for example 8, 15, and 22 yards carry if you have space.

Rule: land in a defined landing zone for each distance.

Advance rules (pick one):

- **Strict:** two in a row at each rung before moving up
- **Normal:** three out of five at a rung before moving up
- **Kind:** best two of five, for rough days

## Landing ladder with one hole

Pick a hole location. Create three landing zones short of the hole that require different carry amounts. Work backward from the pin. This trains green reading plus landing.

## Club ladder

Same distance, three clubs: 8 iron bump, PW, SW. Goal: stop inside the same circle with each. You learn flight versus roll tradeoffs without changing distance.

## Slope ladder

If your practice green has slope, create rungs that force different slopes:

- Uphill chip
- Sidehill chip
- Downhill chip

Pass two of three at each station before moving on.

## Pressure ladder

Add a rule: if you fail a rung twice, drop back one rung. That builds emotional regulation fast.

Connect to [pressure practice: simulate the back nine](/blog/pressure-practice-simulate-the-back-nine/) for bigger stakes versions.

## Worst ball ladder

Two balls per rung, take the worse result. This is advanced. Use it when basic ladders feel easy.

## Scorekeeping

Write:

- Highest rung cleared
- Total balls used
- Miss tendency (short, long, right, left)

Patterns feed [journaling after rounds and range sessions](/blog/journaling-after-rounds-and-range-sessions/).

## Common mistakes

**Changing clubs every rep.** Pick one ladder style per session.

**Landing at the hole.** Land short, let roll work. High handicaps especially benefit from landing discipline.

**No defined circle.** Use a towel, string, or chalk.

## Indoor version

Three tape lines on a mat as landing zones. Roll toward a cup or box. Shorten distances. Keep pass rules.

## Link to full swing spacing

When you understand carry for short shots, full wedge gapping makes more sense. Cross train monthly with [wedge gapping session at the range](/blog/wedge-gapping-session-at-the-range/).

## Time cap

Set a timer. If you do not clear the ladder today, you have a starting point tomorrow. Streaks beat one off hero sessions.

## Partner play

Alternate rungs. If one player fails, the other gets a “steal” attempt. Keep it light. The score still matters.

## Randomize lies safely

Once basics feel stable, draw lie types from a hat: uphill, downhill, ball above feet. Randomness trains adaptability. Keep safety rules: no tight trees, no people in line.

## Speed control within the ladder

Sometimes the miss is not direction, it is pace. Add a rule: ball must land in zone **and** stop within two club lengths. That tightens the ladder without changing distances.

## Using a phone timer

Set sixty seconds per rung attempt. Time pressure reveals default patterns under mild stress, similar spirit to [pressure practice: simulate the back nine](/blog/pressure-practice-simulate-the-back-nine/) but lighter.

## When you get stuck

If you cannot pass rung two for multiple sessions, simplify the landing zone or shorten distance. Pride slows improvement.

## Transfer check

Once a month, take the same ladder concept to the course in a practice round: pick three chip spots, same pass rules, write results. Course grass lies more than mats.

## Pair with stats

If you track up and down rate over time in [tracking progress: what to measure off the course](/blog/tracking-progress-what-to-measure-off-course/), ladder weeks should eventually show up as fewer short game doubles.

## Family time version

If your kids want to join, let them pick silly targets for one rung only, then return to serious scoring on the next rung. Fun plus structure beats chaos.

## Club fitting note

If your wedges have huge sole grind changes, your landing window may change. Re ladder after equipment changes.

## Grass direction

Practice into the grain and downgrain when possible. Roll changes the finish even with the same carry.

## Speed of greens

Fast greens demand smaller landing windows. Slow greens demand more roll planning. Name the green speed you practiced on in notes.

## Link to short game hour

This ladder is a plug in module for [short game practice plan for a focused 60 minutes](/blog/short-game-practice-plan-60-minutes/).

## Mistake: ladder with no puttout

If you ladder chips but never putt, you train half shots. End with a few putts when a cup exists.

## Competitive golfers

Tighten pass rules in season, loosen in off season building phases. Same ladder, different tolerance.

## Solo boredom hack

Listen to a podcast only between stations, not during scoring reps. Attention splits reduce quality.

## Coach checkpoint

If landing is perfect and results are bad, you may have read issues, not technique issues. Get eyes on course slopes when you can.

## Walkthrough: thirty minute ladder

Pick three rungs at 12, 18, and 24 yards carry if space allows. Pass rule: three out of five in the landing zone to advance. If you fail a rung twice, widen the zone slightly, finish the session, and tighten again next time. Write highest rung cleared. Next session, same distances until you clear all three twice in one day. Then move distances by two yards. Small steps beat hero jumps.

If you merge this into a full short game day, use [short game practice plan for a focused 60 minutes](/blog/short-game-practice-plan-60-minutes/) for the surrounding structure.

## Wet turf adjustment

Expect less roll. Landing zones move. Write a wet day note so you do not fight ghosts on dry days.

## Leaf distraction

Fall leaves can hide landing. Use a brighter marker or a string line.

## Sun in eyes

Practice one segment with sun annoyance on purpose if your home course has a painful late afternoon angle.

## Glove on

Keep glove consistent between ladder and course for feel continuity.

## Pre shot routine

Even chips deserve a tiny routine: one look, one practice motion, go.

## Link to bunker

If sand is the fear, alternate weeks with [bunker practice when the course is closed](/blog/bunker-practice-when-the-course-is-closed/).

## Score photo

Snap a photo of your written score if you like digital logs. Fast and simple.

## Teaching kids ladders

Make rungs huge and celebrate loud. Shrink over months.

## Recap

Ladders turn chipping into pass rules and progressive difficulty. They train landing awareness, distance control, and patience under simple constraints. Keep clubs stable for a few sessions before you change equipment variables. Add pressure only when basics feel boring, not when basics feel scary. Log scores so progress is visible. Blend with short game hours and bunker work over a month so the whole around the green game rises together. Adjust for turf, grain, and weather without rewriting your whole identity as a chipper. Small steps beat hero jumps.

Chipping ladders are boring on purpose. Boring reps with clear rules build touch faster than chasing the one skanky highlight shot you tell at the bar.
